II. CASE STUDY & PROJECT DESCRIPTION

Project:
A dual-output research project — a video collage and an accompanying zine — examining the structural parallels between early internet technology and ancient magical traditions. The thesis: both systems operate through symbols, rituals, and hidden knowledge to produce real-world effects.

The organizing framework draws on Arthur C. Clarke's axiom that sufficiently advanced technology becomes indistinguishable from magic — extended here into a genuine comparison of operational logic: HTML and CSS as spellcasting syntax, hyperlinks as ritual pathways, global connectivity as a form of remote viewing. The early web's visual vernacular (low-res graphics, raw markup, blinking GIFs) becomes the aesthetic language for the research.

Tools & Skills
▸ Zine Design — layout, visual hierarchy, print
▸ Video Collage — abstract found footage editing
▸ Archival Research — 90s/00s internet history
▸ Conceptual Writing — technology + occult theory
▸ Image-making — symbolic

Challenge:
The primary challenge was making an abstract theoretical argument legible across two different media simultaneously — without either the zine becoming a dry academic text or the video becoming pure aesthetics with no conceptual anchor.

Research moved across two largely separate bodies of knowledge: the material history of early internet culture (HTML conventions, dial-up interfaces, net art, GeoCities-era web design) and the study of occult traditions — paganism, Wicca, ritual symbolism, and practices like remote viewing. Finding the genuine structural overlap between these two systems, rather than forcing a superficial visual analogy, was the core intellectual task.

The video collage required sourcing and editing abstract imagery from the early internet era and combining it with symbols drawn from magical traditions — binary code alongside runic systems, spellcasting syntax alongside markup language — in a way that was visually coherent and conceptually precise rather than decorative.

Outcome:
The project produced two finished artifacts: a video collage merging early-internet imagery with occult visual language, and a printed zine that operationalizes the research into a tangible, navigable framework — giving the theoretical argument a physical form readers can hold and move through.

The work established a template for subsequent projects: ULTRANET translated the same synthesis of media theory and digital culture into a physical installation, and LOGIN Magazine applied the same research-first editorial logic to independent publishing. Both grew directly from the methods developed here.

Key Takeaway:
This project was the starting point. It established a working method — build a theoretical framework, then translate it into a visual system — and sparked a sustained interest in the internet and technology as the primary themes of the work that followed. Every major project since has been, in some form, an extension of the questions first asked here.
